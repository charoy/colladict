## Introduction

Coordinative artifacts are the modest things that make complex cooperation hold together: labels, forms, folders, tokens, boards, plans, routing slips, notations and other inscribed objects that help distribute tasks and orient action. What is distinctive about the concept is its refusal to reserve analytical attention only for advanced digital systems. In many settings, the practical intelligence of cooperation is carried by ordinary artifacts whose importance is obvious to workers but easy to overlook in formal system descriptions.

These artifacts do not merely record work after the fact. They actively organize it. They indicate what should happen next, how items should move, where attention should go and how dispersed participants can align with one another. Often they are most effective because they are simple, familiar and tightly embedded in local practice.

## Why it matters

In CSCW, coordinative artifacts help ground discussions of collaboration in material detail. They show how cooperative order is delegated into things and how even low-tech artifacts can perform sophisticated organizational work. This insight is especially valuable for design, because it suggests that digital systems should be studied in relation to existing artifact ecologies rather than imagined as total replacements for them.

A critical perspective also highlights their fragility. Because coordinative artifacts are so closely tied to local routines, importing them into new systems or abstracting them into software can strip away the contextual knowledge that made them work. The concept therefore encourages a more careful attention to the humble material forms through which collective action is actually held together.
