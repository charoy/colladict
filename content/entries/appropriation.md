## Introduction

Appropriation is a key concept for any CSCW perspective that takes actual use seriously. Designers may propose functions, workflows and categories, but technologies enter worlds of practice already structured by routines, constraints, histories and local inventiveness. Appropriation names the ways users adapt, bend, reinterpret or repurpose systems as they incorporate them into ongoing activity.

This is not simply misuse or deviation from design. In many cases appropriation is what makes a technology workable at all. Users attach new meanings to features, invent conventions around them, combine them with other artifacts or transform them into tools for purposes never anticipated in requirements documents. Appropriation therefore reveals the incompleteness of design and the continued agency of users in shaping systems after deployment.

## Why it matters

In CSCW, the concept is valuable because collaborative systems rarely survive intact when they meet situated practice. Successful use often depends on flexibility, hackability, reinterpretation and local accommodation. Design that leaves no room for appropriation tends to fail when confronted with heterogeneous organizational realities.

A critical perspective, however, also notes that celebration of appropriation can romanticize the burdens users absorb when systems are poorly matched to work. Not all appropriation is empowering. Sometimes it is compensatory labour, undertaken because institutions offload adaptation onto those with the least formal design authority. The concept remains useful precisely because it keeps both creativity and burden in view.
