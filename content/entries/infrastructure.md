## Introduction

Infrastructure is one of the concepts through which CSCW moved beyond the visible surface of interfaces and applications. It names the standards, classifications, installed bases, routines, maintenance practices and institutional linkages that make collective action possible over time. In everyday use, infrastructure often disappears into the background. It becomes salient when it breaks, when it excludes, or when different systems and communities can no longer align with one another.

What matters here is that infrastructure is not simply a technical layer underneath social life. It is sociotechnical from the start. It is built into habits, organizations, professional divisions of labour and material arrangements. This is why infrastructures are difficult to redesign from scratch: they are not isolated objects but accumulated environments that people inhabit and adapt to.

## Why it matters

In CSCW, infrastructure helps explain why collaboration cannot be reduced to communication tools alone. Cooperative activity depends on installed classifications, compatible records, durable identifiers, repair practices, support staff and expectations about who keeps systems working. These conditions are often taken for granted until coordination fails.

A critical introduction to infrastructure also shifts attention toward maintenance and power. Infrastructures support some forms of work while marginalizing others; they travel unevenly across sites; and they often require extensive invisible labour to remain stable. To speak of infrastructure is therefore to ask what kinds of collective life are being sustained, for whom, and at whose expense.
