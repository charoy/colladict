## Introduction

Distributed cognition is one of the frameworks that most clearly expanded the intellectual horizon of CSCW. Instead of locating cognition exclusively inside the individual mind, it examines how remembering, deciding, calculating and interpreting are achieved across people, artifacts, inscriptions and environments. A chart, a whiteboard, a checklist, a screen, a spoken exchange and a bodily gesture can all participate in a single cognitive accomplishment.

What matters here is not the claim that cognition sometimes spills outward from the mind, but that many practical achievements are better explained at the level of the whole sociotechnical arrangement. A navigation team, a surgical team or a control room is not simply a collection of individuals each doing private mental work; it is a distributed system in which representational labour is shared, stabilized and transformed through coordinated activity.

## Why it matters

For CSCW, distributed cognition provides a strong alternative to design models that treat collaboration as the exchange of already formed thoughts between separate individuals. It suggests that tools and representations are not external supports added after cognition happens; they are part of the process through which cognition is accomplished.

A critical use of the concept also helps resist simplistic narratives about intelligence and automation. If thinking is distributed across people and artifacts, then redesigning a tool is simultaneously redesigning the cognitive ecology of work. This makes questions of representation, delegation and control central to the study of collaborative systems.
