## Introduction

In Colladict, governance is not an external administrative layer added after the intellectual work is done. It is part of the editorial object itself. The way proposals are received, how disagreements are surfaced, who is allowed to reformulate an entry and how decisions are recorded all shape what kind of dictionary can emerge. A critical dictionary cannot treat its own modes of curation as neutral.

This is why contributions enter the project first through GitHub issues rather than direct publication. In practical terms, a proposal for a new entry is submitted to the editorial board through an issue, discussed there, and either declined, revised or accepted for development. If the board accepts the proposal, the proposer receives a limited period of time to complete the entry, in one language or in several languages, before the board reopens the topic for reassignment or further editorial intervention.

The editorial board does not only react to incoming proposals. It can also invite external contributors to write entries when their expertise is especially relevant to a concept, debate or field of literature. Issue-based contributions thus preserve the provisional stage of editorial work: a term can be proposed before it is stabilized, a disagreement can be documented before it is resolved, and a translation can be requested before it exists. The issue becomes a public coordination device between contributors, the board and future readers.

## Why it matters

Treating governance as an entry in the dictionary makes the contribution workflow visible as a conceptual and political question. It foregrounds tensions between openness and curation, discussion and decision, collective participation and authored synthesis. It also clarifies that publication is not the suppression of disagreement, but the result of a documented sequence through which issues are debated by the editorial board, selected, assigned, drafted within a bounded time frame and transformed into public text.
