## Introduction

Making work visible is both an analytical problem and a political one. Cooperative work depends on representations: plans, dashboards, reports, tickets, schedules, records and metrics. These representations allow work to be coordinated, discussed and evaluated across distance and time. Yet they never simply mirror practice. They select, simplify and transform it.

The phrase is especially important in CSCW because the field has repeatedly shown that representations of work are themselves part of the work. Recording, documenting and classifying do not sit outside practice as neutral descriptions. They intervene in what counts, what can be coordinated, who is accountable and which parts of activity remain legible to others.

## Why it matters

In CSCW, making work visible matters because collaborative systems often succeed or fail on the quality of their representations. Too little visibility and coordination becomes difficult; too much formalization and systems flatten situated complexity into misleading abstractions. The challenge is not simply to reveal work, but to decide what form of visibility supports action without erasing what matters.

A critical approach also asks who benefits from visibility and who bears its cost. To make work visible for management, audit or platform governance may mean intensifying surveillance, standardization or self-reporting burdens. The concept remains useful because it keeps alive a necessary caution: visibility is never innocent, even when it is indispensable.
