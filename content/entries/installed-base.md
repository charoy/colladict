## Introduction

Installed base is the concept that prevents infrastructure from being imagined as a blank slate. It refers to everything already in place when a new system is introduced: technical standards, legacy software, paper records, habits of coordination, regulatory requirements, procurement arrangements and institutional compromises. Collaborative systems never arrive in an empty world; they enter environments already thick with prior decisions.

This makes change path-dependent. New components must interoperate with old ones, work around them, layer on top of them or selectively replace them. The installed base is therefore not just a technical constraint. It is a historical accumulation of sociotechnical choices that shapes what kinds of redesign are feasible, affordable and politically acceptable.

## Why it matters

In CSCW, the concept matters because it explains why infrastructure evolves through cultivation more often than through wholesale replacement. Designers and organizations inherit dependencies they cannot simply erase. The installed base carries inertia, but it also carries resources, competences and legitimacy.

A critical use of the concept reveals that installed bases distribute power unevenly. Some groups are better positioned to impose standards or to absorb incompatibilities than others. Appeals to “what is already in place” can function as pragmatic realism, but also as a justification for preserving inequitable arrangements. Installed base is therefore both an analytic description of historical constraint and a reminder that history itself is politically loaded.
