## Introduction

Coordination mechanisms name the means by which cooperative work handles interdependence. They may take the form of schedules, forms, charts, checklists, routing procedures, conventions or specialized artifacts, but they all address the same problem: work in complex settings depends on multiple activities being kept sufficiently aligned without everyone constantly renegotiating everything from scratch.

The concept is important because it treats coordination as materially and organizationally mediated. Dependencies are not managed by communication alone. They are delegated into artifacts and routines that make some next actions obvious, sequence tasks, mark responsibilities or stabilize expectations. In this sense, coordination mechanisms are not abstract principles hovering above work; they are embedded in concrete arrangements.

## Why it matters

For CSCW, the concept offers a bridge between ethnographic studies of situated work and computational system design. It explains why collaborative technologies often succeed not by replacing social organization, but by extending, formalizing or reconfiguring existing mechanisms of coordination. Systems become workable when they fit within, or carefully transform, the means by which dependencies are already handled.

A critical reading, however, notes that mechanisms can oversimplify just as much as they can support. They may privilege one sequence over another, rigidify local practice, or export burdens of compliance onto workers. The concept therefore helps design research ask not only how coordination is achieved, but which form of order is being imposed and who must absorb the resulting rigidity.
