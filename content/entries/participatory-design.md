## Introduction

Participatory design is one of the most distinctive political and methodological traditions connected to CSCW. It begins from a simple but demanding proposition: people affected by technological change should have a substantive role in shaping the systems that reorganize their work and everyday practices. In that sense, participatory design is not just a method for collecting requirements; it is a stance on knowledge, democracy and design authority.

Historically rooted in Scandinavian workplace democracy movements, participatory design challenged the separation between expert designers and passive users. It argued that those who do the work possess situated forms of knowledge that are indispensable for meaningful system design. Workshops, prototypes, mock-ups and collaborative explorations were not merely facilitation devices, but ways of redistributing who gets to define the problem in the first place.

## Why it matters

In CSCW, participatory design matters because collaborative systems inevitably reshape relations of work, coordination and power. Design decisions are never only technical. They affect discretion, visibility, accountability and the distribution of burdens across a collective. Participatory approaches therefore remain central whenever a system is likely to alter the terms on which people cooperate.

A critical introduction also has to acknowledge how easily participation can be domesticated. Many contemporary processes borrow the language of co-design while retaining managerial control over agenda, timing and acceptable outcomes. Participatory design remains analytically useful precisely because it helps distinguish between participation as political redistribution and participation as carefully managed consultation.
