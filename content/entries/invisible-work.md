## Introduction

Invisible work is a foundational concept for critical CSCW because it names an enduring mismatch between what organizations officially count and what cooperative activity actually requires. Every workplace depends on tasks that remain difficult to register as work proper: reminding, repairing, smoothing over tensions, preparing environments, maintaining records, translating categories, helping others recover from interruption, and dealing with the fallout of imperfect systems.

This work is not inherently invisible. It becomes invisible within particular arrangements of accounting, management and design. Some forms of labour disappear because they are gendered, delegated or associated with care rather than expertise. Others disappear because they are distributed, intermittent or accomplished through artifacts that register outcomes but not the work of getting there.

## Why it matters

In CSCW, the concept challenges design approaches that imagine cooperation as a matter of matching tasks to tools. It reminds us that much of the actual burden of coordination lies in background activity that systems can obscure as easily as they can support. If that labour is not acknowledged, technologies frequently intensify it while presenting themselves as efficiency gains.

A short introduction to invisible work therefore opens directly onto questions of value and justice. What counts as contribution? Which forms of labour remain creditable, and which remain silently expected? CSCW keeps this question alive by insisting that systems should be studied not only through what they make possible, but through what kinds of work they make disappear from view.
