## Introduction

Boundary objects are among the most influential concepts to travel from science and technology studies into CSCW. The term names those artifacts, forms, documents, maps, classifications or shared arrangements that permit cooperation among people who do not fully agree on meanings, priorities or practices. What makes the concept powerful is that it does not imagine collaboration as consensus. It starts instead from heterogeneity and asks how coordination remains possible across it.

The object at stake can be interpreted differently by different groups and still function as a point of connection. A patient record, a project plan, a specification, a bug report or a timetable may each do this kind of work. In that sense, boundary objects are not just things that circulate between communities; they are practical devices through which communities manage to remain partially connected without becoming identical.

## Why it matters

For CSCW, the concept helps explain why cooperative systems cannot be designed only around a single unified representation of work. Different participants bring different temporalities, accountabilities and vocabularies, and collaborative artifacts often succeed precisely because they do not eliminate that plurality. They hold together by being interpretable from more than one position.

A critical use of the concept also avoids turning every shared artifact into a success story. Boundary objects can stabilize asymmetries just as easily as they enable collaboration. They may privilege one group's categories, conceal labour required for translation or make conflict appear resolved when it has merely been bracketed. The concept therefore remains useful not only for design, but for asking whose version of coordination gets materialized in the object itself.
