## Introduction

Situated action is one of the concepts that helped reorient CSCW away from purely cognitive or planning-based views of work. Instead of assuming that people execute well-formed plans in stable environments, the concept argues that action is accomplished in relation to concrete situations. People use plans, procedures and rules, but they use them as resources for navigating unfolding circumstances rather than as complete instructions that determine conduct in advance.

This shift had major consequences for the design and study of interactive systems. It suggested that breakdowns in human-computer interaction often arise not because users fail to follow a plan, but because systems are built on impoverished assumptions about what acting in context actually involves. Work is interrupted, repaired, interpreted and resumed through local reasoning that no abstract workflow can fully anticipate.

## Why it matters

In CSCW, situated action remains central because cooperative work is almost always contingent. People must align with others, with documents, with schedules and with material settings that do not unfold exactly as expected. The concept therefore supports a richer understanding of coordination as practical accomplishment rather than rule-following.

A critical use of situated action also resists the fantasy that good design eliminates ambiguity altogether. What it can do, more modestly and more realistically, is support people in handling ambiguity, breakdown and adjustment. That lesson remains especially relevant whenever systems promise frictionless automation for work that is, in practice, deeply situational.
