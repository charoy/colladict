## Introduction

Maintenance and repair direct attention toward the labour required not to invent systems, but to keep them going. CSCW has long shown that cooperative order depends on ongoing acts of adjustment, cleaning, updating, patching, replacing, calibrating and recovering. Systems do not simply run until they fail; they remain operative because people continuously intervene to sustain them.

This perspective is important because dominant stories about technology privilege novelty, deployment and innovation. Maintenance and repair reverse that emphasis. They begin from the observation that breakdown is ordinary and that continuity is an achievement. What appears stable from a managerial or design perspective is often the result of constant care performed by technicians, coordinators, operators, clerks, moderators and other maintainers.

## Why it matters

In CSCW, maintenance and repair help connect infrastructure studies with labour analysis. They make visible the practical and moral work of keeping organizations, devices and information systems usable in the face of wear, contingency and change. This is especially important in collaborative settings where the failure of one element can propagate across many interdependencies.

A critical use of the concept also challenges the political hierarchy that celebrates builders while marginalizing maintainers. Maintenance can be framed as secondary, reactive or unskilled even when it is central to organizational survival. The concept therefore supports a more grounded account of sociotechnical life: less fascinated by clean beginnings, more attentive to the worlds held together through ongoing acts of care and repair.
